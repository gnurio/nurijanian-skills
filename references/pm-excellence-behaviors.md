# PM Excellence Behaviors

Extracted from a survey of designers and developers: "What stands out about the best product manager you've ever worked with? What did they do uniquely well?"

This is the canonical reference for all pm-alignment skills. Each skill draws on the relevant clusters when assessing or coaching a PM.

---

## Cluster 1: Communication

The best PMs treat communication as infrastructure — not a one-time announcement but an ongoing act of clarity-building.

- Communicates context, data, and research clearly; acts like an equal member of the team rather than a director
- Maintains consistency in tone and approach — doesn't oscillate based on mood or pressure
- Translates stress and pressure from leadership into constructive, proactive action plans rather than passing the anxiety down
- Champions the "why" — gets people excited about the impact of building the "what"
- Understands the delineation between Why, What, and How — delivers full context in every conversation
- Understands the difference between product decisions and technical decisions; is responsive to each kind
- Communicates complexity upward (to leadership) and builds consensus downward (with the team)
- Communicates openly — collaborates directly on documents, doesn't hoard decisions
- Constantly re-ups product and process goals; doesn't treat communication as done once

**Anti-patterns:** Communicating inconsistently, letting leadership pressure flow through unfiltered, being unclear about what we're working on and why.

---

## Cluster 2: Decision Quality

The best PMs make decisions that are defensible on paper and trusted by the team — because they're visibly grounded in data and customer reality, not gut or politics.

- Makes customer-centric, impact-focused, logically consistent, and transparent decisions
- Understands how to derive and utilize data properly — doesn't make calls from bad data or no data
- Calibrates risk through scoping and experimentation rather than avoiding risk or ignoring it
- Ruthlessly de-prioritizes unimportant things; stays clear on impact
- Is clear on what we're working on now and next — not just eventually
- Forms coherent hypotheses and opinions based on data-driven analysis; willing to challenge the status quo
- Sees the end picture, but also identifies intermediate stages where the product yields value, feedback, or insights — embraces rework as a tool, not waste
- Understood and practiced "commander's intent" — told talented team the vision and outcome, then got out of the way

**Anti-patterns:** Making decisions for ego or kudos, using data poorly, being unclear about priorities, over-investing in ideas that don't hold up.

---

## Cluster 3: Team Empowerment

The best PMs know the difference between their job and their team's job — and they don't confuse the two.

- Knows when to use decision-making power for good outcomes and when not to — doesn't use it for kudos
- Delegates authority to experts (coders, designers) — sets deadlines and timeboxes, facilitates workshops that end in decisions not talkathons
- Gives designers and engineers enough space to do their actual job — doesn't micromanage based on taste
- Doesn't ask to change designs based on personal preference; fought for the team's autonomy against leadership when needed
- Shortens feedback loops; gets engineers involved in product decisions early and often
- Respects each person's way of working; finds the best path forward for the whole team
- Advocates for the team in the design process and doesn't expect the world in one sprint
- Empowers and includes team members; communicates openly and collaborates on shared documents

**Anti-patterns:** Using authority for ego, micromanaging design, creating long talkathons without outcomes, excluding engineers from early decisions.

---

## Cluster 4: Presence & Execution

The best PMs show up — physically, cognitively, and as collaborators. They do the unglamorous work.

- Shows up, even nearly every standup — is in the mix as much as anyone
- Takes on any busy work that will move things forward (e.g., coordinating and summarizing user interviews, informing stakeholders)
- Is impeccably organized and crystal clear — the opposite is an immediate disqualifier
- Prepared to argue with devs when the situation calls for it
- Helps hone in on parts of a large idea that resonate from a customer and business perspective
- Didn't hold on to the title — shared how they got to insights and priorities with the team (transparent about process)
- "What would it take to just get this part done and ship it?" — relentlessly focused on moving forward

**Anti-patterns:** Disorganized, unclear, absent from key team moments, hoarding the "how we got here" context.

---

## Cluster 5: Research & Domain Grounding

The best PMs are not generalists floating above the work — they're grounded in the actual domain, the actual users, and the actual product.

- Does (and continues doing) market, competitive, and consumer research to align product choices with clearly identified and communicated problem spaces
- Has deep understanding of the existing product that has already been built; considers existing conventions when adding features
- Has domain expertise backed with years of previous field work where possible
- Speaks in the language of impact with immaculate focus on edge cases — thinks about how decisions will scale
- Thinks about what users *may* do, not just what they're *designed* to do — the entire decision space, not just the happy path
- An effective interest-based negotiator

**Anti-patterns:** No ongoing research investment, weak understanding of the existing product, ignoring edge cases, negotiating from positions rather than interests.

---

## Cluster 6: Listening & Openness

The best PMs gather signal from everyone around them — and visibly act on it. They're not performing openness; they're genuinely building on others' thinking.

- Is a great listener — takes all of what their team is saying and turns that into action
- Actively seeks input and makes the process effective for each team member
- Open-minded to exploratory or counter-intuitive approaches
- Not overly invested in their own ideas — treats feedback personally only as a motivator to think harder
- Makes reasonable allowances in time and resource
- Willing to challenge the status quo with a coherent hypothesis, not just intuition
- Challenges engineers when needed — doesn't just defer to technical constraints
- Strong collaborator: always listening to input and suggestions, focused on the solution (not on deadlines or on being right)

**Anti-patterns:** Taking feedback personally, being too attached to own ideas, defaulting to technical constraints without challenging them, appearing to listen but not acting on it.

---

## Cluster 7: People & Care

The best PMs treat people as whole humans, not as execution resources — and this shows up in ways that are hard to fake.

- Treats team members like valuable partners — doesn't speak down to people, doesn't make people feel like junior executors
- Invests in getting the right people involved to succeed — not just whoever is available
- Takes time to support team members who are struggling personally — human first
- Focused on outcomes and the user; willing to fight upper management to create the best product possible
- Spends more time thinking about and communicating *problems* than *solutions* — leaves room for the team to solve
- Thinks more about shipping product than receiving credit — ships and moves on
- Keeps the customer in focus for the whole team — communicates the purpose of the team's work
- Great listener — takes all of what teams are saying and turns that into action to change the team
- Erudition, understanding, seeing the big picture — brings intellectual depth to the role

**Anti-patterns:** Speaking down to people, taking credit, optimizing for their own recognition, keeping the customer invisible to the team, thinking in solutions before problems.

---

## Summary: The Immediate Disqualifiers

From the survey, these are the behaviors that instantly mark a PM as poor (not just imperfect):

1. **Disorganized and unclear** — the best ones are impeccably organized and crystal clear; the inverse is the immediate disqualifier
2. **Overly invested in own ideas** — takes feedback personally, won't change direction
3. **Decision-making for ego** — using authority for kudos, not outcomes
4. **Data blindness** — can't derive or utilize data properly
5. **Micromanaging taste** — asking to change designs based on personal preference, not user need
6. **Inconsistent communication** — tone/approach changes based on pressure; team can't predict how news will land
