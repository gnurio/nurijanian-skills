# Schelling's Focal Points — Source Reference

Source: Thomas C. Schelling, *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960)

## Definition

A focal point (Schelling point) is an outcome that people gravitate toward in coordination problems — not because it is logically superior, but because it is **prominent, unique, or mutually recognizable** enough that each party expects the other to expect it.

"Finding the key, or rather finding a key — any key that is mutually recognized as the key becomes the key — may depend on imagination more than on logic; it may depend on analogy, precedent, accidental arrangement, symmetry, aesthetic or geometric configuration, casuistic reasoning, and who the parties are and what they know about each other."

## Properties That Make Something a Focal Point

### 1. Uniqueness
The signal must be unambiguous. If there are multiple "obvious" choices, coordination fails.

Example: On a map with a single house and many crossroads, 11 out of 11 people who chose the house all met. The 4 who chose crossroads all chose different ones and never met.

### 2. Simplicity
Discrete, qualitative distinctions beat quantitative degrees. "No gas" is a focal point; "some gas with restrictions" is not.

"'Some gas' raises complicated questions of how much, where, under what circumstances: 'no gas' is simple and unambiguous."

### 3. Precedent
What happened before creates a groove both sides can follow by default.

"Precedent seems to exercise an influence that greatly exceeds its logical importance or legal force."

### 4. Symmetry
Mathematical purity (like 50-50) provides a neutral coordination point.

"If all numbers but one represent an asymmetrical split, then sheer mathematical symmetry is a sufficient rule and a supremely helpful one."

### 5. Cultural Salience
Prominence depends on time, place, and who the people are. What works as a signal for one group may not work for another.

"Ordinary folk lost on a plane circular area may naturally go to the center to meet each other; but only one versed in mathematics would 'naturally' expect to meet his partner at the center of gravity of an irregularly shaped area."

## Methods for Finding or Creating Focal Points

### Scan for the Uniquely Conspicuous
Look for the one thing that stands out. The resting place for expectations where one party can say, "If not here, where?"

### Use Precedent and Tradition
Outcomes are often dictated by what has happened before. Historical, literary, legal, and moral precedent provides "authority" for certain limits.

### Apply Symmetry and Simplicity
Mathematical symmetry (50-50 split) or qualitative simplicity ("no gas" instead of "some gas") acts as a magnet for expectations because it is unambiguous.

### Frame the Problem (Set the Stage)
Skillful bargainers formulate a problem to give prominence to a favorable outcome. Choose which data to emphasize, which precedents to bring to light.

"The 'obvious' outcome depends greatly on how the problem is formulated, on what analogies or precedents the definition of the bargaining issue calls to mind."

Example: Including income figures in a tax-split problem shifts the focal point from 50-50 to proportional-to-income, because the income data creates a "strong signal."

### Introduce Noise (Defensive)
To avoid an unfavorable focal point, deliberately introduce irrelevant or confusing data to drown out a strong signal and force retreat to a simpler split.

"The refined signal for the income proportionate split was drowned out by 'noise,' and the cruder signal for equality was all that came through."

### Make the First Move (Unilateral Innovation)
Create a focus by making a visible, unchallenged proposal first. Even a poor or discriminatory signal can work if it is the only one available.

"The strongest argument in favor of R is the rhetorical question, 'If not R, what then?'"

### Use a Mediator
A third party can create a focal point by making a "dramatic suggestion" that fills a "vacuum of indeterminacy."

"The bystander who jumps into an intersection and begins to direct traffic at an impromptu traffic jam is conceded the power to discriminate among cars by being able to offer a sufficient increase in efficiency to benefit even the cars most discriminated against."

## The Cognitive Process

Finding a focal point is not about guessing what the "average person" will do. It is a reciprocal meeting of minds.

"One is not, in tacit coordination, trying to guess what another will do in an objective situation; one is trying to guess what the other will guess one's self to guess the other to guess, and so on ad infinitum."

Imagination is required to select a clue from the concrete details. "Poets may do better than logicians at this game, which is perhaps more like 'puns and anagrams' than like chess."

Empathy (social perception) is the ability to read the same message in a common situation. Coordination depends on a "spark of recognition" passing between players.

## Classic Experiments

| Problem | Result | Why It Worked |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| Meet in NYC, no instructions | Grand Central Station, 12 noon | Conventional priority, landmark uniqueness |
| Name heads or tails | 36 heads, 6 tails | Conventional priority of "heads" |
| Pick the same positive number | 40% chose "1" | "First" = unique among infinite candidates |
| Divide $100 into piles A and B | Vast majority: $50/$50 | Mathematical symmetry |
| Map with one house, many crossroads | 11/11 at house, 4/4 at different crossroads | Uniqueness avoids ambiguity |
| Map with one bridge | 7/8 met at bridge | Unique feature on the landscape |
| Draw a border between two forces | 14/22 X's and 14/23 Y's chose the river | Qualitative geographic boundary |

## Dynamic Properties

- Small concessions are less likely than large ones near focal points.
- Focal points are more persuasive as exact outcomes than as approximations (demanding 50% then compromising to 47% looks like collapse).
- Qualitative principles are hard to compromise — focal points generally depend on qualitative distinctions.
- Breaching a focal point risks collapsing the entire framework of limits ("to collapse it may lead to a jointly less favorable limit or to none at all").

## Universal Focal Points (Zero Shared Context)

Schelling addresses the extreme case: coordinating with an alien civilization. When all cultural context is stripped away, only the physical universe provides clues.

- **Frequency**: The radio emission line of neutral hydrogen at 1420 megacycles — "a unique, objective standard of frequency, which must be known to every observer in the universe"
- **Signal format**: Sequences of small prime numbers or simple arithmetic sums
- **Principle**: When culture cannot help, mathematics and physics are the only remaining magnets

## Key Insight

"The authority is in the expectations themselves, and not in the thing that expectations have attached themselves to."

A focal point works because people expect it to work, and they expect it to work because they expect others to expect it to work. The object itself is arbitrary — what matters is the mutual recognition.
