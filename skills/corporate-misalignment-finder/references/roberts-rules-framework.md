# Robert's Rules Framework for Corporate Misalignment

## Core Principles from Robert's Rules of Order (12th Edition)

### The Balance of Rights
Parliamentary law is built on a careful balance of rights:
1. **Rights of the majority** - to prevail when a decision is made
2. **Rights of the minority** - to be heard, and to force a majority to be at least a majority
3. **Rights of individual members** - to speak, vote, and participate
4. **Rights of absentees** - to be protected by requiring notice for certain actions

### The Deliberative Assembly
A deliberative assembly has these characteristics:
- Free agent: can do what it wants with protection for itself and members
- Basic principle: majority vote decides (more than half of those present and voting)
- Requires simultaneous aural communication (electronic meetings OK, email/chat is NOT deliberative)

### Key Diagnostic Tools

#### 1. Point of Order (§23)
Used when there is a breach of the rules.
- **Purpose**: Enforce rules and protect rights
- **When to use**: Any time a rule is violated
- **Effect**: Chair must rule immediately
- **Appealable**: Yes, to the assembly

#### 2. Parliamentary Inquiry (§33)
Used to ask the chair's opinion on procedure.
- **Purpose**: Clarify process without accusing of error
- **When to use**: Unsure of proper procedure
- **Effect**: Chair gives non-binding opinion
- **Appealable**: No

#### 3. Question of Privilege (§19)
Used when rights or comfort of members are affected.
- **Purpose**: Address urgent matters affecting the assembly or a member
- **When to use**: Discomfort, disorder, breach of rights
- **Effect**: Chair decides if urgent enough to interrupt
- **Types**: Affecting assembly as a whole, or individual member

#### 4. Appeal (§24)
Used to challenge chair's ruling.
- **Purpose**: Let assembly decide on interpretation
- **When to use**: Disagree with chair's ruling on Point of Order
- **Effect**: Assembly votes on whether chair was correct
- **Standard**: Majority vote (or tie sustains chair)

### Common Sources of Misalignment

#### Process Breakdowns
1. **No clear motion on the floor** - Business can't proceed without a motion
2. **Confusion about precedence** - Not knowing which motion is in order
3. **Failure to obtain the floor** - People speaking without recognition
4. **Improper amendment** - Changing motions incorrectly
5. **Unclear voting** - Not knowing what is being voted on

#### Authority Problems
1. **Chair overreach** - Presiding officer making decisions without authority
2. **Unauthorized action** - Doing what bylaws don't permit
3. **Failure to enforce quorum** - Acting without minimum members present
4. **Unclear delegation** - Not knowing who has decision rights

#### Rights Violations
1. **Preventing debate** - Not allowing discussion before vote
2. **Ignoring motions** - Chair refusing to state a proper motion
3. **Denying the floor** - Not recognizing members properly
4. **Intimidation** - Making members uncomfortable participating

### The Order of Precedence (What Outranks What)

From highest to lowest priority (can interrupt/precede lower):

1. **Fix Time to Adjourn** (privileged)
2. **Adjourn** (privileged)
3. **Recess** (privileged)
4. **Raise Question of Privilege** (privileged)
5. **Call for Orders of the Day** (privileged)
6. **Lay on the Table** (subsidiary)
7. **Previous Question** (subsidiary) - close debate
8. **Limit/Extend Debate** (subsidiary)
9. **Postpone to Certain Time** (subsidiary)
10. **Commit/Refer** (subsidiary)
11. **Amend** (subsidiary)
12. **Postpone Indefinitely** (subsidiary)
13. **Main Motion** (lowest)

### Motions That Bring Question Back
- **Take from the Table** - resume suspended business
- **Rescind** - cancel previous action
- **Amend Something Previously Adopted**
- **Discharge a Committee**
- **Reconsider** - reopen decision (only by someone who voted with prevailing side)

### Meeting Types and Their Constraints

1. **Regular Meeting** - established schedule, any business
2. **Special Meeting** - specific notice required, only that business
3. **Adjourned Meeting** - continuation of previous meeting
4. **Annual Meeting** - elections, major business
5. **Executive Session** - confidential proceedings
6. **Electronic Meeting** - requires bylaws authorization, simultaneous audio

### Quorum Requirements
- Minimum members to conduct business
- Usually defined in bylaws
- Without quorum, only motions to establish quorum, recess, or adjourn are valid
- Actions taken without quorum are null and void

### Voting Thresholds
- **Majority vote** (>50% of those voting) - standard for most decisions
- **Two-thirds vote** - required when restricting rights (closing debate, etc.)
- **Plurality** - largest number when no majority (only when bylaws allow)

### Minutes Requirements
Minutes should record:
- Type of meeting (regular, special, etc.)
- Date and time
- Names of presiding officer and secretary
- Whether quorum present
- All main motions (except withdrawn)
- All motions that were not unanimous/consent
- Final disposition of each motion
- Counts of votes if counted or divided

### Prevention of Misalignment

1. **Clear Bylaws** - authority, process, and rights defined
2. **Trained Presiding Officer** - knows rules and stays neutral
3. **Proper Notice** - everyone knows what's being decided
4. **Agenda Adherence** - business conducted in order
5. **Recorded Minutes** - decisions captured accurately
6. **Regular Review** - rules updated as needed
